"Why Your Language Matters Most" A message for parents

[0:00–0:20] — Opening
You want the best for your child. You want them to do well in school, to be confident, to love learning. And you might think the fastest way to get there is to start them on English — or whatever the language of school is — as early as possible.
But research tells us something surprising. The single most powerful thing you can do for your child's education is to make sure they learn to read in your language first.

[0:20–0:55] — The brain argument (simple)
Here's why. When a child learns to read, their brain is doing something incredible. It's connecting the sounds they already know — the words they've heard since before they were born — to symbols on a page.
If those sounds are familiar, that connection happens quickly and naturally. The brain says: I know that word. I know what it means. Now I just need to learn what it looks like written down.
But if a child is learning to read in a language they're still learning to speak — like trying to solve two puzzles at the same time — progress is much slower. And far more children give up.

[0:55–1:30] — What the research shows
Studies from across Africa, Asia, and South America consistently show the same thing. Children who learn to read in their mother tongue in the early years — the first two or three years of school — go on to perform better in all subjects. Including in English and other languages, later on.
They read faster. They understand more. They stay in school longer.
Because once a child truly knows how to read — once that skill is solid — they can transfer it to any language. Reading itself doesn't need to be re-learned. Only the new words do.

[1:30–2:00] — The identity argument
There's something else, too. When a child sees their language written down — when they find stories, books, and learning materials in the words they speak at home — it sends them a powerful message.
Your language matters. Your family's way of speaking matters. You matter.
Children who feel that sense of pride and belonging in the classroom are more engaged, more confident, and more willing to take risks with their learning.
When a child's home language is invisible at school — never written, never read, never celebrated — it can quietly tell them the opposite.

[2:00–2:35] — Practical reassurance for parents
Now, you might be wondering: but won't this slow down their English? Won't they fall behind?
The evidence says no. In fact, the opposite is true. A child with strong mother tongue literacy picks up a second language more easily — because they already understand how reading works. They have the confidence. They have the foundation.
Think of it like building a house. You wouldn't skip the foundations to put the roof on faster. Strong foundations make everything above them more stable.

[2:35–3:00] — Call to action / Close
So what can you do?
Read with your child in your language. Tell them stories. Sing songs. Let them see that their words have value.
